Getting Started

Quantum Computing for Android (aka this app) emulates (almost) the lowest level of a quantum computer, i.e. logic gates. These are also the basic components of classical computers, however, in quantum computers logic gates work somewhat differently, which you can read about more in detail in the next part.
To place a logic gate in the app, first click somewhere on the screen. In the appeared window, you can select a gate and modify its properties. For example, its place or in case of a rotation or U3 gate, the angle of the rotations.

If you are done placing gates, you can execute the circuit, which may take a while if you placed many of them. It is worth taking a quick tour in the settings menu, to see what options are available. You can turn on and off the measurement of any qubit by pressing on their name once. Red/dark-grey qubits are measured, pale-grey qubits are not. Please be aware, that if you choose to ignore any qubit, the state-vector will not be shown.
If you wish to change the order of the gates, or if you want to replace them with a different one, simply click on the gate, and select the desired option in the dialog that opened.

You can also save the circuit you have created, but before you need to select a folder for saving, in the settings. This directory will also be used to save any gates you have created yourself in the gate editor. To open a file, click the folder icon on the bottom left corner of the main screen and select the desired file. The app currently only supports its own file format, which are .qsf files. It is possible, however, to export the circuits into OpenQASM files. These files cannot be opened by the app yet.

Quantum Logic Gates

Quantum logic gates (QLGs) – similarly to classical logic gates – perform some operation on one or more qubits that changes their state. These logic gates can be divided into two main groups, based on how many qubits are involved in the operation. An important property of QLGs is that they are reversible, which means that no information is lost when we use them. From this, it follows that they have an equal number of inputs and outputs.

In order to use these gates on your virtual qubits in this app, you need to choose first a gate then select which qubits should be affected by it.
In addition to predefined gates, the app includes two types of customizable gates, one of them being the Rotation gate, while the other is the U3 gate. The latter is really important, as all other single qubit gates are basically just a variant of U3.
Please note that for now, these gates are ideal, in contrast with real quantum gates that have some noise (a certain probability that they will not work as intended).

Rotation Gates

Support for rotation gates has been removed as it was a nonstandard way to manipulate qubits. You can perfectly replace them with U3 gates. See its matrix below for help.

cos(θ/2) -ei(π/2)sin(θ/2)
ei(φ-π/2)sin(θ/2) eiφcos(θ/2)

U3 Gate

The U3 gate is a unitary gate that has three parameters, hence its name. Using this gate, any single qubit gate can be constructed. It is also possible to create a controlled U3 gate, which can replace any two-qubit gate.

cos(θ/2) -eiλsin(θ/2)
eiφsin(θ/2) ei(λ+φ)cos(θ/2)

Hadamard
The Hadamard gate rotates the qubit along the xz‑axis. This gate brings the qubit into a superposition of 0 and 1.

<Hmatrix>

Pauli gates
The Pauli gates rotate the qubit along the corresponding axes (X, Y, and Z). The Pauli‑X gate is also called NOT.

<Xmatrix>

<Ymatrix>

<Zmatrix>

S and T (phase-shift) gates
These gates change the phase of the qubits (along the Z axis). The S gate is a π/2 phase-shift and the T is a π/4 phase-shift. In order to make use of them, the qubit has to be in a superposition of 0 and 1. In a pure 0 or 1 state, phase-shift has no effect.

<Smatrix>

<Tmatrix>

√NOT
Similarly to the Hadamard gate, the square root of the NOT gate brings the qubit into a superposition of 0 and 1 states, however, with a phase shift of -π/2. Two √NOT gates following each other equal to a Pauli‑X gate, hence its name. This gate is equal to a rotation gate where θ=π/2 & φ=0.

<Qmatrix>

Identity
The identity matrix keeps the qubit in its current state (i.e does nothing).

<Imatrix>


Multi Qubit Gates

Multi qubit gates act on a pair or more qubits, and usually they have one or more control and target qubits. A control qubit influences how the gate will act on the target qubit. The control qubit is denoted by a smaller circle in the app. Sometimes, however, both qubits are equal, eg. in the SWAP gate.

CNOT
It is perhaps the most important gate in working with entanglement on quantum computers. To put things simple, it basically rotates the target qubit around its x-axis if the control qubit is measured 1 right after the application of the gate. It is a controlled Pauli‑X gate.

<CNOTmatrix>

Controlled Y
Similarly to CNOT, it performs a rotation if the control qubit is measured 1, however, around the y-axis. This gate is also a controlled Pauli gate.

<CYmatrix>

Controlled Z
As the above two gates, this gate performs a rotation along the Z-axis if the control qubit is measured 1. Besides being a controlled Pauli gate, this is also a controlled phase-shift gate with an angle π.

<CZmatrix>

Controlled S
Controlled S gate used to perform a relative phase shift of π/2 between the two qubits if the control qubit is 1, to conform to the way Qiskit defined this gate. Since Qiskit changed their definition, this gate now means a phase shift of π/2 on the target qubit.

<CSmatrix>

Controlled T
Controlled T gate used to perform a relative phase shift of π/4 between the two qubits if the control qubit is 1, to conform to the way Qiskit defined this gate. Since Qiskit changed their definition, this gate now means a phase shift of π/4 on the target qubit.

<CTmatrix>

Controlled Hadamard
The controlled version of the Hadamard gate.

<CHmatrix>

SWAP
The swap gate swaps the properties of 2 qubits.

<SWAPmatrix>

Toffoli
The most common 3 qubit gate performs a NOT operation on the target qubit if both control qubits are measured 1.

<CCXmatrix>

Fredkin
This 3 qubit gate is also known as Controlled SWAP.

<CSWAPmatrix>


Bloch Sphere

The state of an individual, non-entangled qubit can be visualized using the so-called bloch sphere. To view the state of one of the qubits in your circuit, click on the axes button in the top right corner and navigate to the desired qubit using the buttons on the bottom. Please note, that the state displayed on the bloch sphere is the state of the qubit before the first multi-qubit gate was applied on the selected qubit.


OpenQASM Export

If you wish to open your circuit in another program, your best option is to export it into OpenQASM. Please note, that the application is currently unable to export any user-defined gate that uses more than one qubit. If the application encounters any such gate, it will leave a remark in the QASM file. Single-qubit user-defined gates will be converted into U3 when saving. Some special gates may also be converted into a combination of other gates, such as the √NOT.


Available settings

Shots
Select how many times the experiment should be executed before displaying the probabilities. You can also switch to statevector mode, where the final statevector of the system will also be shown. In this mode you will get the probabilities that an ideal, noise-free quantum computer would produce.

Threads
Control the degree of parallelization in the program. Increasing this value beyond the available CPU cores does not bring significant performance increase. Some internal calculations are hardcoded to use 4 threads.

Output folder
Select where to find the user-defined gate files and where to export the results and circuit files. It is recommended to create a separate folder on your phone's storage for Quantum Computing.

Optimize the circuit
By optimizing the circuit the app ensures that your circuit uses the least amount of qubits. At least 5 qubits are always in use. The app also includes a different auto-optimization process. This takes effect when the gates and qubits used exceed a certain number, and merges consecutive single-qubit gates (temporarily) to speed up execution.

Scientific notation
Use scientific notation when exporting results into CSV files. This allows a greater range to be saved and smaller file size. However, some programs may not support it. (MS Excel and Google Sheets do)

Separator
Select which character should separate the columns in the CSV files. Most programs use comma or tab, however, some programs or languages can use a semicolon.
