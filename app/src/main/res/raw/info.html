Quantum Logic Gates

Quantum logic gates (QLGs) – similarly to classical logic gates – perform some operation on one or more qubits that changes their value. These logic gates can be divided into two main groups, based on how many qubits are involved in the operation. An important property of QLGs is that they are reversible, which means that no information is lost when we use them. From this, it follows that they have an equal number of inputs and outputs.
In order to use these gates on your virtual qubits in this app, you need to choose first a gate then select which qubits should be affected by it.
Please note that for now, these gates are ideal, in contrast with real quantum gates that have some noise (a certain probability that they will not work as intended).

Rotation Gates

Rotation gates as their name suggests, rotate the state-vector of the selected qubit along a selected axis. Using the standard parametrization of the sphere, we can easily describe these operations. Let's denote θ the angle of rotation around the X-axis of the bloch sphere, and φ the angle of rotation around the Z-axis, where θ ∈ [0, π] and φ ∈ [0, 2π[. The rotation around the Z-axis is also called phase-shift. When you create a rotation gate using the app, always the rotation around the X-axis will be performed followed by the rotation around the Z-axis.

Single Qubit Gates

Hadamard
The Hadamard gate rotates the qubit along the xy‑axis. This gate brings the qubit into a superposition of 0 and 1.

S and T (phase-shift) gates
These gates change the phase of the qubits (along the Z axis). The S gate is a π/2 phase-shift and the T is a π/4 phase-shift. In order to make use of them, the qubit has to be in a superposition of 0 and 1. In a pure 0 or 1 state, phase-shift has no effect.

<Tmatrix>

√NOT
Similarly to the Hadamard gate, the square root of the NOT gate brings the qubit into a superposition of 0 and 1 states, however, with a phase shift of -π/2. Two √NOT gates following each other equal to a Pauli‑X gate, hence its name.

<Qmatrix>

Identity
The identity matrix keeps the qubit in its current state (i.e does nothing).

<Imatrix>


Multi Qubit Gates

Multi qubit gates act on a pair or more qubits, and usually they have one or more control and target qubits. A control qubit influences how the gate will act on the target qubit. Sometimes, however, both qubits are equal, eg. in the SWAP gate.

CNOT
It is perhaps the most important gate in working with entanglement on quantum computers. To put things simple, it basically rotates the target qubit around its x-axis if the control qubit is measured 1 right after the application of the gate. It is a controlled Pauli‑X gate.

<CNOTmatrix>

Controlled Y
Similarly to CNOT, it performs a rotation if the control qubit is measured 1, however, around the y-axis. This gate is also a controlled Pauli gate.

<CYmatrix>

Controlled Z
As the above two gates, this gate performs a rotation along the Z-axis if the control qubit is measured 1. Besides being a controlled Pauli gate, this is also a controlled phase-shift gate with an angle π.

<CZmatrix>

Controlled S
Controlled S gate performs a relative phase shift of π/2 between the two qubits if the control qubit is 1.

<CSmatrix>

Controlled T
Controlled T gate performs a relative phase shift of π/4 between the two qubits if the control qubit is 1.

<CTmatrix>

Controlled Hadamard
The controlled version of the Hadamard gate.

<CHmatrix>

SWAP
The swap gate swaps the properties of 2 qubits.

Toffoli
The most common 3 qubit gate performs a NOT operation on the target qubit if both control qubits are measured 1.
